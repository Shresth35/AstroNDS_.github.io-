Welcome to the ultimate beginner-friendly guide to capturing the night sky. This document covers the essential gear, camera settings, and techniques needed to photograph stars, the Milky Way, and deep-sky objects.Required Gear💡 Essential EquipmentCamera: A DSLR or mirrorless camera with manual mode capabilities.Lens: A wide-angle lens (14mm to 24mm) with a fast aperture (f/2.8 or wider).Tripod: A sturdy, heavy tripod to prevent camera shake from wind.Intervalometer: A remote shutter release to avoid touching the camera during exposures.🚀 Advanced EquipmentStar Tracker: A motorized mount that rotates with the Earth to prevent star trailing.Light Pollution Filter: A filter to block city light glow and improve contrast.Equatorial Mount: A heavy-duty tracking system for deep-sky telescopes.Camera Settings MatrixUse these baseline settings as a starting point for your first night shoot:TargetFocal LengthApertureISOShutter SpeedMilky Way14mm - 24mmf/2.83200 - 640015 - 25 secondsStar Trails14mm - 35mmf/4.0800 - 160030 seconds (Looping)The Moon200mm+f/8.0100 - 2001/125 - 1/250 secondStep-by-Step Workflow1. PlanningCheck the moon phase; a new moon offers the darkest skies.Use apps like Stellarium or PhotoPills to locate the Milky Way core.Find a dark sky location using a light pollution map.2. Focusing at NightSwitch your lens to Manual Focus (MF).Turn on Live View and zoom in digitally on the brightest star in the sky.Slowly turn the focus ring until the star becomes a sharp, tiny pinpoint.Tape down the focus ring with painter's tape to prevent accidental movement.3. Calculating Shutter Speed (The 500 Rule)To avoid blurry stars caused by Earth's rotation, divide 500 by your focal length.Formula: 500 / Full-Frame Focal Length = Maximum Exposure Time (seconds).Example: 500 / 20mm lens = 25 seconds maximum shutter speed.Crop Sensor Note: Multiply your focal length by the crop factor (1.5x or 1.6x) before dividing.4. Post-ProcessingAlways shoot in RAW format to capture maximum data.Use Adobe Lightroom or darktable for exposure, contrast, and white balance adjustments.Use DeepSkyStacker or Sequator to stack multiple images to reduce digital noise.
